## Content for each scenario path

### Path 1: "I recently lost a loved one" (any urgency)

**Action Plan:**
1. Get 10–12 certified copies of the death certificate from the funeral home — you will need more than you expect.
2. Notify Social Security immediately — call 1-800-772-1213. Any payments received after the date of death must be returned.
3. Locate the will or trust — if you don't know where it is, contact the attorney who drafted it.
4. Do NOT distribute any assets yet — even if the will seems straightforward, probate or legal steps must come first.
5. Open an estate bank account to receive incoming funds and pay estate expenses.
6. Identify and secure all property — change locks if needed and notify homeowner's insurance of the death.

**Documents to Find:**
- Will or trust document
- Certified death certificates (get at least 10–12)
- Social Security card
- Medicare and insurance cards
- Financial account statements (bank, investment, retirement)
- Property deeds and vehicle titles
- Life insurance policies
- Last 2 years of tax returns

**Who to Call First:**
- Estate or probate attorney — required before distributing any assets
- Bank or financial institutions — to notify of death and understand next steps
- Life insurance companies — to file claims (there are deadlines)

**Expert Types:** Probate Attorney, Estate Planning Attorney

---

### Path 2: "In a crisis and need immediate help" (extremely urgent)

**Action Plan:**
1. Find out if the hospital admission is classified as "inpatient" or "observation status" — ask the charge nurse directly. This affects Medicare coverage significantly.
2. Ask to speak with the hospital's discharge planner or social worker today — don't wait for them to come to you.
3. Locate the Medicare card and any supplemental insurance cards now.
4. Find out if a Durable Power of Attorney exists and where it is.
5. Designate one family member as the single point of contact for the medical team — multiple voices create confusion.
6. Do not accept the first discharge plan presented without asking questions — you have the right to request more time and evaluate options.

**Documents to Find:**
- Medicare card and any Medicare Advantage or supplement plan information
- Durable Power of Attorney
- Healthcare Proxy / Healthcare Power of Attorney
- Advance Directive / Living Will
- List of current medications and dosages

**Who to Call First:**
- Hospital social worker — ask for them by name, today
- Elder law attorney — if Medicaid, asset protection, or legal documents are a concern
- Geriatric care manager — if you need an independent advocate to evaluate care options quickly

**Expert Types:** Elder Law Attorney, Geriatric Care Manager

---

### Path 3: "Managing ongoing care for a loved one"

**Action Plan:**
1. Request a written care plan from the current provider — if one doesn't exist, ask for it in writing.
2. Understand exactly what Medicare is covering vs. what you are paying out of pocket — most families are surprised by the gaps.
3. Research whether your loved one may qualify for Medicaid — even if they have assets, planning now protects more than waiting.
4. Evaluate whether the current care setting is the right long-term fit given projected needs and costs.
5. Confirm all legal documents are in place — Durable POA, Healthcare Proxy, and an up-to-date will.
6. Create a contact list of all providers, insurers, and agencies involved in care — keep one copy at home and one digital.

**Documents to Find:**
- Current care agreements and care plan
- Medicare Summary Notices
- Insurance policies (Medicare supplement, long-term care)
- Durable Power of Attorney
- Financial account summary

**Who to Call First:**
- Geriatric care manager — for independent care coordination and options assessment
- Elder law attorney — for Medicaid planning before assets are spent down
- NJ SHIP counselor (free) — for Medicare questions, call 1-800-792-8820

**Expert Types:** Geriatric Care Manager, Elder Law Attorney, Medicare Specialist

---

### Path 4: "Getting affairs in order"

**Action Plan:**
1. Make sure a Durable Power of Attorney is in place — this is the single most important document if something unexpected happens.
2. Complete a Healthcare Proxy / Healthcare Power of Attorney designating who makes medical decisions.
3. Create or update a will — if there is a trust, make sure it is properly funded.
4. Create a simple one-page document listing all accounts, policies, and where to find everything — leave it somewhere your family can find.
5. Review all beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and life insurance — these override the will and are often outdated.
6. Tell at least one trusted person where your important documents are kept.

**Documents to Find/Create:**
- Durable Power of Attorney
- Healthcare Proxy / Healthcare POA
- Will or Trust (and confirm trust is funded)
- Beneficiary designation forms for all retirement accounts and life insurance
- Account and policy summary document

**Who to Call First:**
- Estate planning attorney — to draft or update documents
- Financial advisor — to review and update beneficiary designations

**Expert Types:** Estate Planning Attorney, Financial Planner

---

### Path 5: "I want to plan ahead" or "I'm an aging individual planning for myself"

**Action Plan:**
1. Understand your Medicare enrollment windows now — missing the Initial Enrollment Period results in permanent premium penalties.
2. Research long-term care costs in your area — in New Jersey, nursing home care averages over $12,000/month.
3. Evaluate whether long-term care insurance makes sense for your situation — it is far cheaper to buy at 60 than at 70.
4. Make sure all legal documents are in place: Durable POA, Healthcare Proxy, will or trust.
5. Have a direct conversation with family about your wishes, and make sure at least one person knows where your important documents are.
6. Consider a free Medicare counseling session through NJ SHIP — call 1-800-792-8820.

**Documents to Find/Create:**
- Durable Power of Attorney
- Healthcare Proxy / Healthcare POA
- Will or Trust
- Medicare card and current coverage summary
- Long-term care insurance policy (if you have one)

**Who to Call First:**
- Elder law attorney — for Medicaid planning and legal documents
- Medicare specialist — to review current or upcoming coverage
- Financial planner specializing in eldercare — to evaluate long-term care funding

**Expert Types:** Elder Law Attorney, Medicare Specialist, Financial Planner
